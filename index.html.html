The six units — what you must know

Each card lists the ideas the exam actually tests, in plain English.

Unit 15–10%

Basic Economic Concepts

  • Scarcity: we have limited resources but unlimited wants. Every choice has a cost.
  • Opportunity cost = the value of the next best thing you give up.
  • PPC: points ON the curve = efficient. Inside = unemployment of resources. Outside = impossible right now. The curve shifts out with more resources or better technology.
  • Comparative advantage: the producer with the LOWER opportunity cost should make that good. Countries trade because of this.
  • Demand shifts (not price!): tastes, income, related goods, expectations, number of buyers. Supply shifts: input costs, technology, taxes/subsidies, expectations, number of sellers.
  • A price change moves you ALONG a curve. A shifter moves the WHOLE curve.
Key terms: scarcity · opportunity cost · PPC · absolute vs. comparative advantage · equilibrium · shortage · surplus
Unit 212–17%

Economic Indicators & the Business Cycle

  • GDP = C + I + G + Xn — everything produced inside a country in one year.
  • NOT in GDP: used goods, financial transfers (stocks), intermediate goods, non-market work, and things made in other countries.
  • Unemployment types: frictional (between jobs), structural (skills no longer needed), cyclical (recession). Natural rate = frictional + structural. At full employment, cyclical = 0.
  • Unemployment rate counts only people looking for work. Discouraged workers are NOT counted — this makes the rate look better than reality.
  • Inflation: measured with CPI. It hurts savers and lenders; it helps borrowers (they pay back cheaper dollars).
  • Real vs. nominal: real = adjusted for inflation. Always compare economies with REAL GDP.
Key terms: GDP · CPI · inflation · frictional / structural / cyclical · labor force · recession · expansion
Unit 317–27%

National Income & Price Determination

  • AD shifters: changes in C, I, G, or Xn. AD slopes down because of the wealth, interest-rate, and exchange-rate effects.
  • SRAS shifters: input prices (wages, oil), productivity, inflation expectations. LRAS is vertical at full-employment output.
  • Recessionary gap: output below full employment. Inflationary gap: output above it.
  • Multiplier: spending is multiplied. Spending multiplier = 1/(1−MPC). Tax multiplier is smaller and negative: −MPC/MPS.
  • Fiscal policy = government spending and taxes. Recession → spend more / cut taxes. Inflation → spend less / raise taxes.
  • Long-run self-adjustment: with no policy, wages eventually change and SRAS shifts to bring output back to full employment.
Key terms: AD · SRAS · LRAS · MPC · MPS · multiplier · output gap · fiscal policy · automatic stabilizers
Unit 418–23%

Financial Sector

  • Bond prices and interest rates move in opposite directions. Memorize this.
  • Real interest rate = nominal rate − inflation. Real is what matters for saving and investing.
  • Money has 3 jobs: medium of exchange, unit of account, store of value.
  • Banking: banks keep some deposits as reserves and lend the rest. Money multiplier = 1 / reserve requirement. A $1,000 deposit with a 10% requirement can create up to $10,000 of money.
  • Money market: vertical money supply, downward money demand. The Fed shifts MS to change the nominal interest rate.
  • Monetary policy: to fight recession → buy bonds / lower rates (expansionary). To fight inflation → sell bonds / raise rates (contractionary). With ample reserves, the Fed mainly adjusts administered rates like interest on reserves.
  • Loanable funds market uses the REAL interest rate. Savers supply; borrowers demand.
Key terms: reserves · demand deposits · open market operations · federal funds rate · money demand · loanable funds
Unit 520–30%

Long-Run Consequences of Stabilization Policies

  • Biggest unit on the exam. It connects everything from Units 3 and 4.
  • Phillips curve: short run — inflation and unemployment trade off (SRPC slopes down). Long run — no trade-off; LRPC is vertical at the natural rate.
  • AD shifts = movement ALONG the SRPC. SRAS shifts = the SRPC itself SHIFTS.
  • Quantity theory: MV = PQ. If money grows too fast for too long, you get inflation. "Inflation is a monetary phenomenon."
  • Deficit = one year of overspending. Debt = all deficits added up.
  • Crowding out: government borrowing raises the real interest rate → less private investment → slower growth.
  • Economic growth comes from more capital, better technology, and better-educated workers (productivity). Growth = LRAS and the PPC shift right.
Key terms: SRPC · LRPC · natural rate · quantity theory · velocity · deficit vs. debt · crowding out · productivity
Unit 610–13%

Open Economy — International Trade & Finance

  • Balance of payments: current account (goods, services, income) + capital & financial account (assets, investment). They balance each other.
  • Exchange rate = the price of one currency in another currency.
  • Appreciate = currency gets stronger. Depreciate = currency gets weaker.
  • Demand for dollars comes from foreigners wanting U.S. goods and assets. Supply of dollars comes from Americans buying foreign things.
  • The chain you must know: currency depreciates → that country's exports get cheaper → exports ↑, imports ↓ → net exports ↑ → AD ↑.
  • Interest rates and capital flows: money flows TO the country with the higher real interest rate. That country's currency appreciates.
Key terms: exchange rate · appreciation · depreciation · current account · financial account · capital flows · net exports

The graphs you must draw from memory

FRQ graders give points for correct labels and correct shifts. Practice drawing each one until it takes you under 30 seconds.

Production Possibilities Curve

Use for: scarcity, opportunity cost, efficiency, economic growth.

Label: both axes with the two goods, and the curve.

Reserve Market

Use for: modern Fed policy with AMPLE reserves. The Fed sets administered rates to move the policy rate.

Label: Policy rate, Quantity of reserves, DR (down, then flat), SR (vertical), PR₁. With ample reserves, SR crosses the FLAT part of DR — so changing reserves barely moves the rate. The Fed moves the rate by changing administered rates (like interest on reserves), which shifts the flat part of DR up or down.

AD–AS Model

Use for: recessions, inflation, fiscal & monetary policy. The most-used graph on the exam.

Label: Price level, Real GDP, AD, SRAS, LRAS, PL₁, and Y (use Yf for full employment).

Money Market

Use for: monetary policy and the NOMINAL interest rate. Money supply is vertical.

Label: Nominal interest rate, Quantity of money, MS (vertical!), MD.

Loanable Funds Market

Use for: saving, borrowing, crowding out, and the REAL interest rate.

Label: REAL interest rate (not nominal!), S of loanable funds, D of loanable funds.

Phillips Curve

Use for: the inflation–unemployment trade-off, short run vs. long run.

Label: Inflation rate, Unemployment rate, SRPC, LRPC vertical at the natural rate.

Foreign Exchange Market

Use for: appreciation and depreciation of a currency.

Label: the vertical axis as "other currency per this currency" — e.g., euros per dollar for the dollar market.

Bank Balance Sheet (T-account)

Use for: banking questions. You read these; you may fill in numbers.

Remember: deposits are a LIABILITY for the bank. Only EXCESS reserves can be loaned out.

The #1 way students lose FRQ points: missing labels. Every graph needs labeled axes, labeled curves, and dashed lines to the equilibrium values. When something shifts, draw the new curve with a new label (AD₁ → AD₂) and an arrow showing the direction. An unlabeled graph earns zero, even if the idea is right.

FRQ strategy — how to earn the points

The free-response section is 1/3 of your score. Graders follow a rubric. Give them exactly what the rubric asks for.

1 · Answer the verb

Each question starts with a command word. Do exactly what it says — no more, no less.

IdentifyGive the answer in one short phrase. No explanation needed.
DefineSay what the term means.
CalculateShow your work and give a number with units ($, %).
ExplainGive the answer AND the reason, as a chain: "X rises, so Y rises, so Z falls."
Show / DrawMake a fully labeled graph with the change marked by arrows.

2 · Graph like a grader

  • Label BOTH axes, EVERY curve, and equilibrium points with dashed lines.
  • Shifts get a new curve with a new name (AD₁ → AD₂) and an arrow.
  • Use the right graph: money market = nominal rate; loanable funds = real rate.
  • Even if the question does not ask for a graph, sketching one helps you think.

3 · Use the chain of reasoning

Most "explain" points are earned with a cause-and-effect chain. Practice writing them fast:

  • Fed buys bonds → money supply ↑ → nominal interest rate ↓ → investment ↑ → AD ↑ → real GDP ↑ and price level ↑.
  • Government deficit ↑ → demand for loanable funds ↑ → real interest rate ↑ → private investment ↓ (crowding out).
  • Dollar depreciates → U.S. exports cheaper for foreigners → net exports ↑ → AD ↑.

4 · Manage the clock

  • Use the 10-minute reading period to plan all three answers and sketch graphs.
  • Spend about 25 minutes on FRQ 1 (10 points) and 15 minutes each on FRQs 2 and 3.
  • Answer every part. A blank earns zero; a short honest attempt can earn points.
  • Write short and clear. Graders reward correct chains, not long paragraphs.

5 · Sentence frames — copy these patterns

You do not need fancy English on this exam. Fill in the blanks with economics, and you earn the point.

  • When ___ increases, ___ decreases, because ___.
  • The ___ curve shifts right / left. As a result, ___ rises and ___ falls.
  • The central bank should buy / sell bonds. This increases / decreases the money supply, so the nominal interest rate falls / rises.
  • Formula: ___. Work: ___ × ___ = ___. Answer: $___ / ___%.
  • The (currency) will appreciate / depreciate, because the demand for / supply of it increases / decreases.
  • The opportunity cost of ___ is ___, because that is what must be given up.

Practice FRQs — write by hand, then self-score

These mirror the real exam: one long (10 points), two short (5 points each). Write your answers on paper with a pen, draw every graph, then open the scoring guide and check yourself point by point. Give the long one 25 minutes and each short one 15.

FRQ 1 · Recessionary gap, fiscal policy, and crowding out LONG · 10 points
The economy of Country Z is in a recessionary gap. The unemployment rate is 9%, and the natural rate of unemployment is 5%. The marginal propensity to consume (MPC) is 0.9.
  1. Draw a correctly labeled AD–AS graph for Country Z. Show the current price level PL₁, current output Y₁, and full-employment output Yf.
  2. Identify one fiscal policy action the government could use to close the gap.
  3. Suppose the government increases spending by $20 billion. Calculate the maximum possible change in real GDP. Show your work.
  4. The government borrows the $20 billion. Using a correctly labeled loanable funds graph, show the effect of this borrowing on the real interest rate.
  5. Based on your answer in (d), explain what happens to private investment.
  6. Based on the change in the real interest rate in (d), will Country Z's currency appreciate or depreciate? Explain.
Show the scoring guide (self-score)
  • (a) 3 pts — One: axes labeled "Price level" and "Real GDP" with downward AD, upward SRAS, vertical LRAS. Two: Y₁ shown LEFT of Yf. Three: PL₁ marked at the AD–SRAS intersection with dashed lines.
  • (b) 1 pt — Increase government spending OR cut taxes (either one earns the point).
  • (c) 2 pts — One for the multiplier: 1 ÷ (1 − 0.9) = 10. One for the answer: $20 billion × 10 = $200 billion increase. You must show the work.
  • (d) 2 pts — One for a labeled loanable funds graph (REAL interest rate on the vertical axis). One for demand for loanable funds shifting RIGHT with the real interest rate rising (r₁ → r₂).
  • (e) 1 pt — Private investment falls, because borrowing is now more expensive. (This is crowding out — naming it helps but the explanation earns the point.)
  • (f) 1 pt — Appreciates. The higher real interest rate attracts foreign financial capital, which increases demand for Country Z's currency.
FRQ 2 · Monetary policy and bond prices SHORT · 5 points
The central bank of Country X operates in a banking system with limited reserves. Inflation in Country X is well above its target.
  1. Should the central bank buy or sell government bonds on the open market? Identify the action.
  2. Draw a correctly labeled money market graph showing the effect of the action in (a) on the nominal interest rate.
  3. Explain how the change in the nominal interest rate affects aggregate demand.
  4. What happens to the price of previously issued bonds as a result of the change in interest rates?
Show the scoring guide (self-score)
  • (a) 1 pt — SELL bonds (contractionary policy to fight inflation).
  • (b) 2 pts — One for a labeled graph: "Nominal interest rate" (vertical), "Quantity of money" (horizontal), vertical MS, downward MD. One for MS shifting LEFT (MS₁ → MS₂) with the nominal rate rising.
  • (c) 1 pt — Chain: higher interest rate → borrowing is more expensive → investment (and interest-sensitive consumption) falls → AD decreases.
  • (d) 1 pt — Bond prices FALL. Interest rates and bond prices move in opposite directions.
FRQ 3 · Foreign exchange and net exports SHORT · 5 points
Country M's currency is the mark. Consumers in Europe strongly increase their demand for goods made in Country M.
  1. Draw a correctly labeled graph of the foreign exchange market for the mark. Show the effect of the European demand for Country M's goods.
  2. Based on your graph, does the mark appreciate or depreciate?
  3. Explain the effect of the change in (b) on the price foreigners pay for Country M's exports.
  4. Explain the effect of the change in (b) on Country M's imports.
Show the scoring guide (self-score)
  • (a) 2 pts — One for a labeled graph: vertical axis "euros per mark," horizontal axis "Quantity of marks," upward S, downward D. One for demand for marks shifting RIGHT (Europeans need marks to buy Country M's goods), with the exchange rate rising.
  • (b) 1 pt — The mark APPRECIATES (its price in euros rises).
  • (c) 1 pt — Exports become MORE expensive for foreigners, because each mark now costs more euros.
  • (d) 1 pt — Imports INCREASE, because a stronger mark buys more foreign currency, making foreign goods cheaper for Country M's consumers.
